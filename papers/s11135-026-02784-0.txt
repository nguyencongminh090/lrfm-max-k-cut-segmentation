Abstract
Developing new marketing methods to understand customers’ needs is of vital importance
in a highly competitive market. The customer segmentation aims to divide the customers into disjoint groups of self-similar individuals to apply specific marketing strategies
to each group. Assigning each customer to the most appropriate group is a challenging
task in large datasets. In this paper, we propose a graph-based approach to the customer
segmentation problem by representing each customer as a vertex and two vertices are
connected by a weighted edge according to the recency, frequency and monetary values
associated with each customer. The main advantage of this graph representation is that
we design a procedure to build a reduced graph with fewer vertices and edges, such that
clustering this smaller graph is equivalent to clustering the customer dataset in the original
graph. It clearly provides an advantage in dealing with large instances, and we demonstrate this equivalence by proving two theorems. We applied our methodology to synthetic
datasets randomly generated and to a real-world dataset available in the UCI Machine
Learning repository. In all cases, we compared the results of our method to the results
of clustering algorithms of the literature in terms of the silhouette index. In particular,
we considered the results for the real-world dataset reported in Rungruang et al., Expert
Systems With Applications 237 (2024) 121449. The computational results show that our
method outperformed the other methods in the majority of the instances.
Keywords Customer segmentation · RFM analysis · Graph-based clustering · Maximum
k-cut problem

1 Introduction
In a highly competitive market, developing new marketing techniques is necessary to
understand customers’ needs. Before applying marketing techniques to their customers, the
challenge is to clearly differentiate customers’ behaviours in a massive dataset and provide
an adequate strategy to each group of self-similar individuals. In this scenario, grouping
customers that share similar characteristics, such as purchasing patterns, is an important
step. The Customer Segmentation Problem (CSP) consists of partitioning the dataset of an
organization’s customers into groups so that customers of the same group share common
characteristics. Many studies have shown that customer segmentation enables businesses to
enhance customer loyalty and retention, and helps identify the value of each customer, see
for instance the recent papers of Pai et al. (2025) and Rungruang et al. (2024), and the references therein. A widely used strategy to solve the CSP is to represent each customer by its
purchases in terms of recency, frequency, and monetary (the so called RFM representation)
and apply a clustering method from that representation, that is, combining RFM analysis
with a clustering method is one of the most used techniques to groups customers into groups,
as demonstrated in the following papers (Cheng and Chen 2009; Chen et al. 2012; Christy
et al. 2021; Doğan et al. 2018; Rungruang et al. 2024). The K-means technique is among
the most widely used procedure for clustering due to its ease of application and its ability to
explore large and complex solution spaces efficiently, especially for NP-hard combinatorial
optimization problems where finding the absolute optimal solution is computationally prohibitive for large instances. The solution provided by the K-means method is known to be a
high-quality suboptimal solution, that is, there is no guarantee that the obtained grouping is
the best possible. In this sense, exact methods for segmenting customers are desirable, but
they are very difficult to apply in an environment with a large number of customers due to
the high computational time required for their response.
To address the CSP, this paper proposes the use of an exact clustering mathematical
model, known as the maximum k-cut problem in the optimization literature, for clustering
customers by means of the combination of the RFM model and a graph theory perspective.
To the best of our knowledge, the use of graph theory to address the customer segmentation problem when it is modeled based on the RFM score represents a novel approach
in the literature. Graph-based modeling opens new perspectives for analyzing customer
behavior through the application of network analysis metrics, such as centrality, community
detection and spectral methods, which may be useful for generating rankings and identifying the most relevant customers within the database. Besides, the proposed methodology offers significant computational advantages, as it enables a substantial reduction in the
problem’s dimensionality, regardless of the total number of customers present in the dataset.
This dimensionality reduction allows the adoption of an exact optimization technique on a
smaller, more tractable instance. Furthermore, from the optimal solution of this auxiliary
problem, we demonstrate that it is possible to reconstruct the solution space of the original
formulation and recover the globally optimal solution to the original problem. The proposed
framework thus contributes to enhancing computational efficiency while maintaining solution optimality in large-scale customer segmentation scenarios.
The main contribution of this article is related to the graph-based approach and how it
provides a method for obtaining optimal solutions for clustering customers when a large
number of customers need to be grouped in the CSP. The graph-theoretic perspective plays

a crucial role in the proposed method, as we mathematically prove that large instances can

the UCI Machine Learning Repository. Our approach was compared to other clustering
methods in the literature, including K-means, Gaussian Mixture Models, and a hierarchical

The paper is organized as follows. In Sect. 2, we present the theoretical background
of the paper by describing the graph theory perspective of the CSP by means of the RFM
model and the classical combinatorial optimization problem known as the maximum k-cut
problem. In Sect. 3, we show how to reduce the original graph to an equivalent smaller one
and how we use it to solve the maximum k-cut problem. We also state and prove theoretical
results that form the basis of our method. A summary of our methodology is also presented.
In Sect. 4, the computational results for the synthetic datasets and the real-world dataset
are presented, along with discussions on the practical implications associated with the realworld dataset. Finally, Sect. 5 presents our conclusions and perspective on future works.

2 Background
We explore here the main concepts behind our methodology to obtain a customer
segmentation.

2.1 RFM model
To segment customers into clusters, we need to identify characteristics that allow us to
distinguish them. To this end, we use the customer value analysis method known as RFM
analysis (Cheng and Chen 2009; Hughes 1994). Although there is a wealth of information
about customers that companies can collect, the advantage of the RFM analysis, according
to Cheng and Chen (2009), is that it identifies the characteristics of customers and differentiates them using only three variables, described in the sequel (Cheng and Chen 2009; Christy
et al. 2021):
1. Recency (Ri ): the time interval between the latest consuming behavior of customer i
and the present. The shorter this interval is, the bigger the value of Ri .
2. Frequency (Fi ): the number of customer i purchases in a fixed period. The higher this
number is, the higher the value of Fi .
3. Monetary (Mi ): the total amount of money spent by customer i over the whole time
period considered. The higher this value is, the higher the value of Mi .
Each variable is ordered in ascending order and divided into T equal groups. Then, for each
customer i, we assign an integer value ranging from 1 to T for each variable Ri , Fi and Mi .

So, each customer i is associated with a three-dimensional score (Ri , Fi , Mi ) that we refer
to as (R, F, M)-score of customer i. Note that there are at most T 3 distinct (R, F, M)-scores.

2.2 The maximum k-cut problem
Based on the (R, F, M)-scores of each customer, we show how customers are compared to
each other and divided into groups using a graph approach. First, we need to introduce the
max-k-cut problem.
Consider a simple weighted graph denoted by G = (V, E), where V = {1, 2, . . . , n} is
the set of vertices and E is the set of edges eij connecting vertices i, j ∈ V , with |E| = m.
Each edge eij ∈ E has a non-negative weight wij . Given the parameter k ≥ 2, the maxk-cut problem consists of partitioning the set of vertices V into k disjoint subsets, say
V1 , V2 , . . . , Vk , so that the sum of the weights of the edges connecting vertices belonging
to different subsets of the partition is the largest possible. It is an NP-hard combinatorial
optimization problem (van Dam and Sotirov 2016).
Variations of this problem can be obtained by imposing constraints on each part Vi , for
i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , k}. For instance: (i) if each Vi induces a connected subgraph, then we have
the connected max-k-cut problem (Healey et al. 2024; Hojny et al. 2021; ii) if |Vi | is at most
a given integer number, then we are dealing with the capacitated max-k-cut problem (Gaur
et al. 2008). Also, a wide variety of practical applications can be tackled via the max-k-cut
problem and its variations, such as in the electricity market (Ambrosius et al. 2020; Hojny
et al. 2021), forest planning (Carvajal et al. 2013), scheduling (Carlson and Nemhauser
1966), and clustering (Poland; T. Zeugmann 2006). Concerning the strategies to solve the
max-k-cut problem, we refer the reader to the recent papers (Fakhimi et al. 2023; Healey
et al. 2024; Hojny et al. 2021) for approaches based on mathematical formulations and
relaxations. Heuristic methods have also been proposed to provide good solutions to the
max-k-cut problem (Gaur et al. 2008; Ma and Hao 2017; Zhu et al. 2013).
We consider the following mathematical formulation of the max-k-cut problem in terms
of a binary quadratic optimization (BQO) problem. The decision variables xil represent
whether a vertex i belongs to or not to Vl , that is, xil = 1 if vertex i ∈ Vl and xil = 0 otherwise. The mathematical formulation (BQO) is

3 Research methodology
The underlying rationale of our methodology is that customers sharing identical (R, F, M)scores can be consolidated into a single vertex, with the corresponding adjustments applied
to the incident edges so as to preserve the structural properties of the graph. Procedure 1
details how this reduction is performed on the dataset without loss of relevant information,
while Theorems 1 and 2 establish that any solution obtained from the reduced dataset can be
consistently mapped back to the original problem without compromising optimality. Section 3.1 describes how a dataset represented as a graph can be substantially reduced to a
smaller graph with fewer vertices and edges through the application of Procedure 1. Section
3.2 introduces Procedure 2, which demonstrates that the customer segmentation solution
derived from the reduced graph can be consistently transferred to the original graph. In
particular, a solution to the original dataset can be readily recovered by disaggregating the
merged vertices. Finally, Theorems 1 and 2 prove that, when the maximum k-cut problem
is employed to solve the reduced instance of the customer segmentation problem, the solutions corresponding to the reduced and original graphs are equivalent, thereby preserving
optimality under the maximum k-cut formulation.

contain thousands of customers, finding the optimal solution to the associated max-k-cut
problem (with a high number of vertices and edges) using model (BQO) may be computationally infeasible. For this reason, we propose constructing another graph from G, denoted

Additional comments about the relation between G and G′ for the CSP are worth mentioning. When G is given as input to the max-k-cut problem (BQO), we expect that vertices with
equal (R, F, M)-scores are assigned to the same group (or cluster) in the optimal solution.
Unfortunately, it is not generally possible to guarantee that an optimal solution to the maxk-cut for the graph G has this property. However, we prove that such a solution always exists
and that our technique always leads to it, for which we give a more precise definition in the
sequel to simplify the presentation of our arguments in Theorems 1 and 2.
Definition 1 (Segmentation property) We say that a partition of the vertices of G has the
segmentation property if the vertices having the same (R, F, M)-score lie in the same group.
Definition 2 (Segmentation solution) A segmentation solution to problem (BQO) is a feasible solution that has the segmentation property.
The existence of an optimal segmentation solution is proved in Theorem 1 by showing that,
if you find an optimal solution violating this property, it is possible to make some local
changes in this solution in such a way that we keep the optimal objective function value
and rearrange vertices with equal (R, F, M)-scores to the same group, i.e., we obtain a new
optimal solution that respects the fact that vertices with equal (R, F, M)-scores are always
in the same group.
Theorem 1 Consider graph G, built as described in Sect. 2.2, given as an input to (BQO).
Then, there is at least one optimal segmentation solution for (BQO).
Proof Suppose that x∗ is an optimal solution to (BQO). If x∗ is a segmentation solution,
then the result is proven. Now, consider that x∗ is not a segmentation solution, that is, there
are vertices with the same (R, F, M)-score but belonging to different partition sets.
Let (R, F , M ) be an arbitrary score for which this happens, and let us fix a set Vp of the
partition containing a vertex associated with the score (R, F , M ). We denote this vertex
associated with the score (R, F , M ), that lies in the set Vp , by i. We will conclude that any
other vertex associated with the score (R, F , M ) can be moved to the set Vp and we still
have a global optimal solution. This way, we can find a global optimal solution in which all
the vertices associated with the the score (R, F , M ) are grouped in the set Vp .
Indeed, let j be any other arbitrary vertex associated with the score (R, F , M ) that lies in
a set Vq of the partition, distinct from the set Vp . Let S be the sum of the weights of the edges
connecting vertices that lie in different sets of the partition but are not related to i or j. Let
Sp be the sum of the weights of the edges connecting the vertex i with vertices that lie in a
set distinct from the set Vp of the partition and let Sq be the sum of the weights of the edges
connecting the vertex j with vertices that lie in a set distinct from the set Vq of the partition.
Then the value of the objective function in this optimal solution is S + Sp + Sq .
Considering that i and j are not connected by an edge and share the same neighbors, if
we move the vertex i from the set Vp to the set Vq , the value of the objective function in the
resulting partition is S + Sq + Sq . Since S + Sp + Sq is the value of the objective function
in a global optimal solution, we conclude that

3.3 Summary of our methodology
We summarize the methodology in seven steps. Consider a dataset composed of n customers. We propose a method for customer segmentation using the RFM analysis and clustering by solving the max-k-cut problem related to the dataset. In the sequel, we describe our
graph-based methodology.
● Split the data (recency, frequency, and monetary value), sorting them in ascending order
into T equally spaced intervals, and assign an integer in the range 1 to T to each variable
Ri , Fi , Mi for each customer i ∈ {1, . . . , n}.
● Determine the possible values of k (the number of clusters in which the customers will
be segmented) according to the appropriate criteria, depending on the particular application.
● Build the corresponding weighted graph G on n vertices with the customer dataset. Each
customer i is a vertex of G, and two customers i and j are connected by an edge with
weight wij = |Ri − Rj | + |Fi − Fj | + |Mi − Mj |.
● Reduce G to the graph G′ by merging vertices with the same (R, F, M)-score and updating the edge weights as previously described in Procedure 1. The graph G′ has n′ ≤ n
vertices.
● For each one of the selected values of k, solve the corresponding max-k-cut problem
′
through the formulation (BQO), given G′ with n′ vertices as an input. Let x∗ ∈ Rn ×k
be the optimal solution found for the reduced graph.
● Obtain a segmentation solution x∗ ∈ Rn×k to the problem (BQO) with graph G given
as input using Procedure 2.
● Choose the best value of k through some measure of clustering performance (Karanikola
et al. 2021) or other criteria that may be more suitable for a given practical application.

4 Results and discussion
Our computational experiments were conducted on an Intel Core i7 processor operating at
3.4 GHz, equipped with 64 GB of RAM and running the Ubuntu Linux operating system.
The algorithms were implemented in Python 3.12.2, and Gurobi Optimizer version 11.0.2
was employed to solve formulation (BQO) under its default parameter settings.
This section is organized into two subsections. Section 4.1 evaluates the proposed methodology on three synthetic datasets. For a fixed T in the set {3, 5, 7}, the R, F, and M values
for each customer were randomly generated from a discrete uniform distribution over the
interval [1, T]. To assess the effectiveness and stability of the method, instances comprising
100, 1000, and 10000 customers were considered. We compare our results with the Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) and K-means clustering available in the scikit-learn Python
library. Section 4.2 investigates the performance of the methodology on a well-established
benchmark dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository, which has been widely

three other methods: K-means and Hierarchical approaches as presented in Rungruang et al.
Rungruang et al. (2024), and GMM clustering method. Additionally, we present a businessoriented analysis of the results achieved in Section 4.2.1 using our proposed method.

4.1 Synthetic datasets
To evaluate the robustness and sensitivity of the max-k-cut method for customer clustering,
three synthetic datasets were generated, each representing (R, F, M)-scores of customers.
Dataset of sizes 100, 1000, and 10000 customers were considered. For each dataset size,
(R, F, M)-scores were generated from a discrete uniform distribution over the interval [1, T],
where T = 3, 5 and 7. This experimental design enables a systematic assessment of the
method’s performance under different data scales and score ranges. Additionally, for comparison purposes with the proposed graph-based approach, two other clustering methods —
GMM and K-means clustering — were applied to the same datasets, varying the number of
clusters from 2 to 10, and then we compute the silhouette index for each grouping.
The silhouette index (Rousseeuw 1987) is a metric used to assess the quality of a clustering by considering both the internal cohesion of the clusters and the separation between
them. For each vertex, it calculates how close this vertex is to others in the same cluster
compared to vertices in neighboring clusters, giving us the individual silhouette index of
each vertex. The average of these individual values yields the overall silhouette score. The
closer this average value is to 1, the better the clustering, indicating that the data is well
grouped and well separated. Values close to 0 suggest overlapping clusters, and negative
values indicate poor clustering.

4.2 A real-world dataset
In order to evaluate the usability of the proposed method, we used the Online Retail II dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository. This widely recognised dataset, employed
in several studies, including (Rahim et al. 2021) and (Rungruang et al. 2024), encompasses
transactions from a non-store online retail operation based in the UK, specializing in the
sale of unique all-occasion giftware, primarily for wholesalers. The period considered spans
from December 1, 2009 to December 9, 2011, totaling 1,067,371 transactions and comprising eight variables, including Customer ID, InvoiceDate, Quantity, and Price.
Before applying our method, we preprocessed the data. During the preprocessing phase,
we removed entries with missing Customer IDs, negative values in Quantity and/or Price, as
well as duplicate records. As a result, the dataset was reduced to 79,104 transactions made
by 5878 unique customers. Extreme values were intentionally retained, as the RFM methodology seeks to capture behavioral variability across the customer base, including atypical
or extreme purchasing patterns. Such observations may represent strategically important
customers, and their removal could lead to the loss of valuable business insights. Table 1
presents descriptive statistics of the recency, frequency and monetary variables for all 5878
customers after processing the data.
After data preprocessing, the RFM analysis was performed for each of the 5878 customers. For each customer i, the frequency (Fi ) is defined as the number of transactions made by
i in the considered period; the monetary value (Mi ) is calculated as the sum of the monetary
transaction values (quantity x price) for i; and the recency (Ri ) represents the elapsed time
(in days) between the date of the last purchase made by i and the last purchase in the entire
dataset. Recency was transformed so that a lower value indicates a better outcome, aligning
it with the other variables (frequency and monetary value).
Thus, by adopting T = 5, each customer is assigned to an (R, F, M)-score, with the
three variables (R, F, and M) receiving a score from 1 to 5 based on the quintiles (division
of the dataset into five equal parts) of each variable. A score of 1 represents the worst case,
while 5 represents the best case. The choice of five scores to RFM methodology proved to
be appropriate, as using a smaller number of classes could result in the aggregation of customers with distinct profiles, whereas adopting a larger number of classes would increase
fragmentation, making interpretation and practical application more difficult. Furthermore,

Remark 2 The optimal objective function value of the formulation (BQO) increases as the
number k of clusters increases, but it does not have any implications for the clustering quality. This happens because a partition of the vertices into k distinct groups is a particular case
of a partition of the vertices into k + 1 groups, one of them having no vertices assigned to
it. Hence, when we look for the optimal objective function value with more clusters available, more vertices can be assigned to distinct clusters and thus more edge weights can be
considered in the objective function of the formulation (BQO), increasing its optimal value,
when compared to the optimal value when a smaller value of k is considered.

4.2.1 Business analysis
The computational results obtained from our method indicate that segmenting the customers in the Online Retail II dataset into either two or four distinct groups yields the best
performance in terms of the silhouette index. Here, we describe some statistics obtained for
each group in the cases of two and four groups, and evaluate which configuration is more
appropriate from a managerial and business intelligence perspective.
Table 7 presents a descriptive statistics for the RFM variables when the dataset is divided
into two clusters.

From Table 7, we see that customers in Cluster 1 take about 325 days, on average, to
make a new purchase, have low buying frequency (less than two purchases on average), and
have low monetary value spent over the considered period. Therefore, they are currently
customers who contribute a low income to the company.
On the other hand, on average, customers in Cluster 2, when compared to customers
in Cluster 1, take considerably less time to make a new purchase, have higher buying frequency, and greater monetary value spent over the considered period. Therefore, they are
currently a group of more loyal customers and generate higher income for the company.
However, even after identifying two very distinct groups of customers, some questions
about the customers’ behavior still remain. For instance, considering Cluster 1, we are not
able to differentiate customers who could be stimulated to make more frequent purchases
from those who have possibly been lost to competitors. In Cluster 2, we are not able to
distinguish the most loyal and profitable customers from those who could be the target of
marketing strategies to increase their buying frequency and the amount of money spent at
the company.
We understand that these questions are better answered when we divide the customers
into four distinct groups. Indeed, in this case, we are able to identify more customer segments, which helps us analyze their behavior and relationship with the company in more
detail. Thus, dividing customers into four distinct groups can provide more meaningful and
actionable insights for enterprise applications.

problems. Our approach combines the RFM analysis with the max-k-cut problem. Each
customer is represented by a vertex of a graph G, and the edge weights are the Manhattan

distances between the vectors corresponding to the customers’ (R, F, M)-scores. To segment
the customers into k disjoint groups, we must solve the corresponding formulation (BQO)
of the max-k-cut problem.
However, customer datasets may contain thousands of customers, and solving the formulation (BQO) when G is given as input may be computationally infeasible. In fact, in our
computational experiments on a real-world dataset, in Section 4.2, the graph G had 5878
vertices and 16, 874, 125 edges, and it was not possible to run the corresponding max-k-cut
problem on our computer, for any value of k.
This is why, in our method, we introduced the reduced graph G′ of G, which has a considerably smaller number of vertices and edges, independent of the number of customers.
We then analyzed the auxiliary problem obtained when G′ is given as input in formulation
(BQO), proving, in Theorem 2, that, from any optimal solution to this problem, we can easily retrieve a segmentation solution to the original problem, when G is given as input, via
Procedure 2.
To validate the proposed methodology, we applied the max-k-cut method to synthetic
datasets of different sizes and values of the parameter T (the range of the RFM scores),
which did not appear to be affected by the T value and proved to be competitive with the
well-known K-means method. Next, we applied it to the Online Retail II dataset from the
UCI Machine Learning Repository. After preprocessing the data, we obtained a dataset containing the (R, F, M)-scores of 5878 customers. The initial computational experiments with
small instances, using only the first 40, 45, and 50 customers of this dataset, already suggested the advantage of using our method instead of solving formulation (BQO) with the
original graph given as input. Indeed, besides our method provides a segmentation solution
for each problem, for k > 2, the computer processing time is significantly smaller when we
use the reduced graph.
The full problem, with all 5878 customers, in its turn, was solvable only through our
method, passing the reduced graph G′ as input to the formulation (BQO). We solved the
associated max-k-cut problem for k = 2 to 10 and calculated the silhouette index of the

13

A. L. Corrêa Vianna Filho et al.

resulting clusterings of the 5878 customers, identifying that the highest silhouette indexes
were obtained for k = 2 and 4, in this order.
Nevertheless, to choose the number k of clusters, we argue that other criteria, in addition to cluster quality metrics, should be considered, depending on the practical application
we are working with. In fact, in Section 4.2.1, we analyzed the resulting clustering of the
customers for k = 2 and k = 4 through the minimum, mean and maximum values of the
variables recency, frequency and monetary value of each cluster.
The conclusion was that dividing the customers into four groups enabled us to identify
other segments of interest that were not observable in the division into two groups and could
be targeted by more personalized and effective marketing strategies. Therefore, in our case,
despite k = 2 yielding the highest silhouette index, k = 4 should be preferred because the
resulting clustering is more meaningful and valuable from the business perspective.
We highlight that the methodology presented in this article can be applied in a more general scenario, that is, in a scenario where more than three variables are available, not only
the RFM scores. Examples of these more general scenarios are the models for customer
segmentation with scales for the RFM variables adopting different values for T Doğan et al.
(2018), Panuš et al. (2016), Ramkumar et al. (2025) or which incorporate more variables to
the RFM model, such as the LRFM, RFMTC, RFMD and RFMT models (Chang and Tsay
2004; Noori 2015; Yeh et al. 2009; Zhou et al. 2021). In general, suppose that one wishes
to classify objects in a system consisting of q variables, say V ar1 , V ar2 , , V arq , instead
of only R, F and M. For a fixed T, each variable V ari may assume values from 1 to T. The
same graph theory approach can be used in this setting, and using the Procedure 1 of our
method on this dataset would generate a reduce graph with at most T q vertices. It is clear
that for big values of T and q, obtaining the optimal solution to the corresponding maximum k-cut problem can be hard, from the computational point of view, even after applying
our reduction technique. Still, in these situations, heuristic and/or metaheuristic methods
can be applied to obtain good quality solutions to the customer segmentation problem in a
acceptable computational time. However, note that the proposed mathematical approach is
the same in both situations, which makes the developed method very useful even in different situations. Future works could focus on using our method in practical applications that
employ these more general settings.
